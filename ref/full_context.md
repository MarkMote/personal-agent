# On passion and who i am
I've said something, for a long time now. i used to say it more longer ago, when i was choosing what to do 10+ years ago: there will be three great changes in my lifetime: life extension, a human mars mission, and artificial intelligence. If we are lucky, 4. We discover alien life.  
I always used that as a justification for aerospace. That being the path that resonated with me. That we could leave the confines of the earth. That we could make humans fly like gods. Go to other planets, and answer the big questions about life and the universe. 
I decided i wanted to go to JPL in undergrad and worked out a way to do it through persistence in grad school. I got the offer, but there was another part of life i would have to give up: trying this new thing, being an euntrepreneur, learning to create change in the world in a real and meaningful way. It was an adventure. There were people with this knowledge of how to just do things, and i would never forgive myself if it didnt figure that out too. If I didnt try to do something great, at least once, what kind of life would that be. 
I had great prospects graduating. The professor at Stanford I had worked for was now director of autonomous vehiles at NVIDIA ,and he was buliding a team (before their stock exploded), I had gotten the offer to come work at JPL and all the other internships i did. the priciple rocket landing engineer at spacex reached out to me personally when i graduated to ask if i wanted a tour. I was in a great position then though i knew so little. Ironically, now i know 10x as much as i did then, but think im in a much worse position. 

I started with an idea i cared about: safe autonomy. It was before llms. I wanted to create technology that saved lives, prevented suffering. I wanted to crack vision, the big problem that we humans dedicate most of our brains to. Mars could wait, JPL could wait. But some life paths are attractive. I learned to listed to the YC motto: "make something people want". thats great, and eventually we used that to pivot to something profitable, but less fulfilling. Likewise at roostr, i was enticed by big hard problems and learning to be really competient at doing things. But Im learning that "make something people want" only influences the supply side of the economics, you also have to make something you want. Thats the balance id like to find now. To have financial security to start a family. To help my retiring dad, and let him see me be sucessful. But also to do the things that i feel pulled by, not pushed toward. Career has always been important to me, its where we spend most of our time and its how we give back to the world. In the big picture, im thrilled to be in the position im in. That my passions are for things that the world does care about. I like building robots and spacecraft, and building buinesses, not playing video games or eating fancy food. it should be a big leg up in life, but i need to recover from some risk taking a bit i think.

## An impressive way I've tackled a problem in a new way
I started my Ph.D. in safety for robotics but wanted to do something interesting and out-of-the box when I started. I noticed a big problem with robotics was that at a planning level, robots are told to avoid collisions but have no good plan of action for when they're inevitable. I created the first optimal way let robots plan with collisions. It helps with safety, but also it lets them bounce to improve performance. 
I also contacted a professor at Stanford (now the director of AV research at NVIDIA) and did a collaboration with his lab over the summer on the idea. We put out a journal paper and made this video: [https://youtu.be/4kOOn6TPuDI](https://youtu.be/4kOOn6TPuDI)
