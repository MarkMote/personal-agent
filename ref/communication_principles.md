# Communication Principles

## Core Premise

**Serious people optimize for clarity and alignment early. Wasting time is rude; asking direct questions is not.**

If someone reacts negatively to reasonable questions, that's a signal to downgrade the opportunity.

---

## On Asking About Compensation

**At senior/founder/specialist levels, asking about comp early is normal and expected.**

It's bad taste to:
- Lead with money before any professional context
- Sound transactional or desperate
- Treat comp as the only dimension

It's NOT bad taste to:
- Ask for ballpark alignment once mutual interest exists
- Frame as preventing misalignment and wasted time
- Use ranges rather than demands

### Good Phrasing

> "Just to make sure this is directionally aligned, what range does the role typically land in?"

> "Before we go too far, it would help to sanity check compensation expectations so we're not misaligned later."

> "I want to respect everyone's time — can you share the rough compensation band?"

---

## On Practical Questions (Location, Timeline, Scope)

Founders, VCs, and serious operators **expect** strong candidates to ask about:
- Economics (cash, equity, ownership)
- Location / time commitment
- Decision rights and scope
- Capital availability (for FIR/EIR)

If you don't ask, you look naive, not noble.

---

## Mental Model

Don't think of this as negotiating. Think of it as **constraint validation**.

You're checking whether this path is feasible within your real-world constraints. That's rational behavior, not rudeness.

---

## Rules

1. Clarity early beats politeness theater
2. Asking ranges is normal at senior levels
3. Money is a parameter, not a moral signal
4. Filter fast when inbound — your time has opportunity cost
5. Preserve optionality without overcommitting
6. Avoid environments that punish honest questions
7. Signal seriousness through precision, not vagueness

---

## Why This Matters More For You

Because you are senior, technical, a former founder, and capital-constrained on timeline — you should be **more upfront than average**, not less.

Junior candidates can afford ambiguity. You cannot. Your opportunity cost is real.

---

## Hidden Rules of Senior Hiring

**Information asymmetry is leverage.** Don't volunteer constraints (timing, finances, desperation). Let them infer strength.

**Process control > offer optimization.** Multiple processes converging beats serial pipelines. Delay Tier-1 until calibrated.

**First impression defines your ceiling.** Companies categorize you in the first 1-2 conversations. Early narrative quality > later technical brilliance.

**Recruiters optimize for close rate, not your outcome.** Friendly, not aligned. Treat as info channels, not advisors.

**Equity is where value transfer happens.** Always ask: fully diluted shares, valuation, refresh policy, acceleration, liquidation prefs.

**Titles are leverage instruments.** Affects future recruiter quality, internal authority, comp ceilings, fundraising credibility.

**You're evaluated for risk, not just ability.** Calm judgment and self-awareness often beat raw brilliance.

**Optionality compounds.** Goal is multiple credible options arriving simultaneously. Negotiation power comes from alternatives.

**Time pressure degrades decisions.** Protect the buffer. When runway shortens, you disclose too much and rationalize bad paths.

**Trajectory > role.** Every decision: "Does this increase or decrease probability of Tier-1 access in 2-5 years?"
