### On the Ending
1. **Why did Pytheia shut down?** You had $25k MRR enterprise customer (Lippert), 3 customers total. What happened? Did funding not come through? Did the co-founders want out? Did the market not materialize?
There were a few factors that led to the shutdown. Full story: 
- Location split
	- Matts Girlfriend moved to new jersey, he wanted to move with her
	- Ben and i moved to San Francisco to focus on the startup 
- Fatigue: 
	- We had been through so many pivots and such a long journey at that point that even once we got the contract, our hearts werent in it. We had all drifted so much from the initial vision to make it work. 
- Contract: may want to down play this or skip over? 
	- Lippert CEO discovered the contract was made without his knowledge. Though the people that bought loved what we were giving them, they wanted to cancel 
- Ben had a hard breakup: biggest factor overall. Ben realized that it would be impossible (in his eyes) to pursue both startup sucess and having a relationship and a family at once. He chose family and wanted stability. My reaction to this was: yeah of course, that makes total sense. No hard feelings, we had a great journey and now we will move on to something else. And that was right. There is no shame in quitting something that isn't working for you. 
	- note: there is some irony in this of course. He saw family and statups are totally incongruent. My opinion was that I still wanted to keep doing startups. I met the girl I would later marry a week after that conversation, and we got married a year later.  
- Matts opinion: not super happy about it shutting down, but accepting. 
- Since i wanted to keep doing a startup and they didnt, they went to go work for a larger startup in new york (around series A  i think, much more stability). Still working at same company together to this day. For what its worth, they are at a "text to sql" company, and i think its a huge waste of their talent. 

My next month
- Ben and i were in SF at the time. The accelerator program that had funded us last year was doing another batch, so we went to their office every day to hang out. 
- Thats where i met jacky
- Jacky was working on something similar to us, at least somewhat: he was building something for business automation, he had left flexport with the idea to spin out a product that did weekly business reviews. 
- Coincidentally, Jacky was also CEO, Jacky also had a CTO, that CTO was also named Ben, his Ben quit in the same month because he wanted something more stable too. 
- I spent the next month evaluating two prospects: Jacky and his startup, and another friend i had who had just created a startup with the idea of using agents to help automate engineering tasks. A kind of cursor for engineering. 
- This startup was called Artifact.engineer. We agreed to 50-50 split of the company with me as CTO if it worked out. He later got into YC and has raised ~3M, but that was after I left. 
- In the end, though i was much more in love with the Artifact IDea. Jacky and I clicked more. My honest take is that the other person did not have a great idea of what they were doing. They weren't a particularly good builder, and they didnt understand startups that well, however i did know the chance of suceess was likely higher in the sense that this person would be very good at raising money. But I've always been against that, I beleived in bootstrapping and i ultimately choose founder-fit over idea-fit.

### On the Pivots
6. **Which pivot was hardest?** AV → Robotics → Restaurants → Pricing → Demand. Was any transition particularly painful or contentious?
Yes moving out of robotics adjacent i think was ultimately a mistake, at least for me. The AV Robotics, and restaurant route were all fundamentally the same tech and were all geared at moving to the same ultimate direction: out of the bad 3d perception for robots. A real time semantic oracle. A shared reality that would make the world safer. It felt like we were building the beginnings of an "internet for robots". 
- Part of the pivot was that LLMs were just coming on to scene and we wanted to be building with them 
- Part of the pivot was very sensical: initial idea had failed, we were a solution in search of a problem for a while. best to just scrap everything and start over. 
- but llms was too short term thinking: multimodal, and different areas of cv had gotten huge. 
- Impossible to really know what you got wrong in hindsight. 
Ultimately the pivot worked, we started making sales right after and we ultimatly started making a lot but it was at the expense of passion.
In hindsight, here is what i would do again: 
- at start: likely should have just gotten jobs, built a nestegg and some big company experience first. could have joined once we had secured first large robotics contract. We probably learned more the way we did it, im glad it happened but wouldnt do it that same way again. 
- no results after 1 year, stop, go somewhere to get jobs. Try again in a couple years. should have been quicker to know it wasnt working, or to be persistent enough to wait for when timing caught up. 

Another mistakes: 
- We overestimated our ability to sell the first version to the government at first. The DOT has access to all these traffic cameras, they have high enough quality their operators information they need. We did a lot of customer discovery in this area and learned that the DOT was monitoring all these cameras with people. human beings looking at cameras trying to find wrecks and traffic flow. they were working with a company in NC that was doing a small amount of computer vision, but was charging them a ton for it, and with clearly inferior tech. thought this would be an easy in, and if it were a company, then it would have been. But these were government workers. Their jobs were secure, if we saved them money it didnt benefit anyone really. they didnt really care about their jobs. They were hugely risk averse. they strung us along for months, while we were waiting for a contract. If we could have got it it would have been great: we get access to their cameras, we can sell the data, we can pay for inference by selling to them. The prototypes worked. But they ultimately didnt care. too much red tape. Wasted time. 
- Overindexed on advice early on. Wanted to read every book, talk to every person i knew that had founded a company, but i learned the following
	- advice is state dependent: life is about tradeoffs, if they dont know you personally and cant see what side of the tradeoff you are too extreme on, it could be bad advice
	- dont take advice from anyone you wouldnt trade places with 
	- the right advice is either highly simple or highly specific. experience is hte best teacher and you should never trade advice and theory for real action. 
- thought it woudl be easier. We initially had this idea that we would finance the computer vision company by creating a parking app, this was before they were common plance and it was easy to build. 
- Funding can be helpful. we had very principled reasons for wanting to bootstrap, but of of the ideas likey would have worked if we just raised on them. 
- should have better laid out failure conditions. Hire-fast-fire fast is great advice, not just for hiring. it works for products too. small quick experiemnts that are default dead until proven otherwise is so often the way to go. 

One thing i did right
- these were occasionally very stressful times. Startups are a rollercoaster. I realized things were getting particularly tense one time. That the stress was too high, that our relationships were suffering. We were friends before this and would work better as friends. I created a "state of the business" meeting every 2-3 weeks, on friday we would drink coffee, sit inside, have a very open discussion about where we were, what we were happy with, what we didnt like in the past couple weeks. and very honestly how we felt. I realized that its not always convienient to bring these things up, and if ou are working with peoeple that are more agreeable in terms of personality, the risk is that they build up and turn to resentment. Having dedicated time where things get said was extremely cathardic and helped a ton wit hthe relationship. We made it kind of fun to, it was taking a bit of time off and we got chick-fil-a right afterwards. I think thats the reason why we are still such good friends today.

### On Technology

A patent actually would have been very easy, Matts dad was a patent lawyer. It may have looked great on paper but at the end of the day, it didnt create any real value. We werent optics driven we were results driven. Having the patent would have gone zero in termsof making customers happy. 
We eventually just retired the repo. Ben didnt want to go open source. In startups, the conventional wisdom is "if you arent ashamed of it, you waited too long to ship". but also if thats the case, someone clean code obsessed was not wanted to make public. Plus after a year or so, a lot of the the tech caught up.

Not sure if we mentioned it but we did once sell a simplified version of our tracking tech to a robotics company for 2k. one time deal, not core ip and something we could do in a few days.
