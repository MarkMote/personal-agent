What is your company going to make? Please describe your product and what it does or will do.
We are building a platform that will allow self-driving cars to take advantage of external sensors.
A perception system is the set of sensors, hardware, and software that a car uses to identify what else is on the road, where things are, and how they are moving. Today, automotive manufacturers include redundant perception systems on cars to improve performance.
We will provide the same functionality as a redundant system, but without the extra sensors and hardware. We will do this by letting cars and traffic cameras talk. Specifically, we will use traffic cameras and vehicle telemetry as data sources, then we will use this data to build a common world model that will be shared with cars via the cell network. Instead of adding more sensors to your car, you get access to the resources of every other car and traffic camera in the network. This gives you redundancy from multiple visual perspectives, which is more robust. It also allows a car to see things outside of its direct line-of-sight.

Who are your competitors? What do you understand about your business that they don't?
Mobileye (most feared) offers full onboard, not shared, perception suites for automated driving. They also share some data between vehicles for HD mapping purposes, and could move into sharing real-time perception information (why we fear them).
Continental, Bosch, and Aptiv provide ADAS and perception solutions for automakers, but are more likely to be customers than competitors for our solution.
HERE, Inrix, Wejo, and Otonomo aggregate vehicle telemetry data, but currently only provide that data for live traffic and historical analytics. Nvidia and Woven Planet (a Toyota subsidiary) recently acquired Deepmap and Camera, companies that provide HD mapping for autonomous vehicles. These don't provide real-time perception solutions at the moment, but are the closest capability-wise to becoming direct competitors (aside from Mobileye).
What do we understand that they don't:
1) All self-driving cars are fundamentally trying to understand the same world. You can solve the problem poorly for each vehicle on its own, or pull together resources to get a much better solution. Shared HD static mapping has been successful for this reason, and we think it could be done for real-time perception as well.
2) Mounted traffic cameras are highly undervalued. Whereas one car may need a dozen sensors, one traffic camera can cover dozens of cars.
3) External perception is technically feasible. The biggest concerns with external perception are latency and accuracy - we’ve done some proof-of-concept work that leads us to believe it’s technically feasible. Recently, there has been a lot of advancement for low-latency, large scale computing (AWS Wavelength).
4) Shared real-time perception has value, even at a small scale. The information provided is supplemental, and if the commitment is low enough, people will use any edge they can get.

It is the only approach to solving the perception problem that involves using sensors and computation that is not attached to the vehicle that they are operating on.  

Currently, all perception systems live on the vehicle that they are doing the perception for. A basic set of sensors doesn’t work as well as it needs to, so performance is usually improved by adding more sensors to the vehicle. However, while an improvement, this also doesn’t work as well as we need it to. Adding more sensors is costly and improvements incremental because: (1) there is only so many sensors you can afford to add and (2) with the new sensors placed next to the old ones, you are looking at the same things from the same angles, and are prone to many of the same errors (e.g. something is blocking the car’s field of view, it is seeing something from a strange angle, or there is glare).

The current approach makes sense if you are developing in isolation, but on a crowded road, you may have Cars A, B and C (and so on) all looking at the same things, individually trying to figure out what they are looking at, all the while there are traffic cameras viewing the entire scene from above. Trying to solve the same problem over and over for each car, with no one talking to anyone else, is a completely unnecessary limitation. It is like Wikipedia only letting you see the articles that you have personally authored.

Currently, the way that cars perceive the world is a lot like the way human drivers do: they have a set of sensors on the car (the eyes), and they process all of the information from those sensors with an onboard computer (the brain) in order to figure out what exists around them. 
However, this doesn’t work as well as we need it to, so in more recent years, the trend has been to increase reliability by allocating more sensors and more compute to this task, and even to contain a fully redundant perception system on the car. This is like having a passenger observing the road and telling the driver what they see (unlike humans, computers can share visual information in real time). 
This indeed shows some real benefits. 
Not only is this costly, but that even with these benefits, it still doesn’t give us the performance we need. 
One reason for this is that the “passenger” suffers from the same limitations as the driver in terms of limited perspective and line of sight. That is, if the first set of sensors is being blocked by another vehicle, or is experiencing glare, or is viewing an object from an unusual perspective, then the second set of sensors is doing all of these things too.  
The prevailing opinion seems to be to just add more and more sensors onto the vehicle to overcome these challenges, along with improving the software. 
This seems to make sense, at least until one considers that the car is not isolated from the rest of the world. 
You don't need to keep stacking more sensors onboard the vehicle, you need to learn to use the ones off it. 
In reality, most of the sensors that a vehicle could use are not located on that vehicle, but on all of the other vehicles around it, and on the infrastructure around it. 
For instance, under the current paradigm, you may have Cars A, B, and C (and so on) all driving next to each other, all observing the same scene, and all solving the same problem individually, all the while, there are traffic cameras overviewing the entire scene from above. 
Not only this, but each car may have a fully redundant perception system that they use to solve the same problem again. 
What is ridiculous about this is that no one is talking to anyone else about what they see. 
There is absolutely no reason to limit what you can see to what you have onboard, when you could be using every other sensor from every traffic camera
