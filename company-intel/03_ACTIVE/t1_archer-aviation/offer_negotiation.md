## Friday Call with Dennis (Mar 7)

### Purpose of the Call
Dennis is gauging enthusiasm and checking for concerns. This is a relationship call, not a negotiation. Be warm, be genuine, don't ask for anything yet.

### Talking Points

**Be positive about the role and team:**
"Really enjoyed the onsite. The team is strong and the problem space is a great fit for my background. The case study conversation was one of the best technical discussions I've had in this search."

**On timeline:**
"I'm wrapping up a couple of other conversations this week and next. I want to give you a fully committed answer, not a rushed one. I should have clarity by the end of next week."

**If he asks about concerns or what would help you decide:**
This is where you can plant the location seed without formally negotiating. Something like: "Honestly, the role itself is exactly what I want to be doing. The main thing I'm thinking through is the move to San Jose. I'm pretty rooted in New York. I know Eddy and I talked early on about potential location flexibility, and that's something I'd want to explore."

This lets Dennis think about it over the weekend without you making a formal ask. If he responds positively ("yeah, we can probably work something out"), that's a huge signal. If he says "we really need people on-site," you know the terrain.

**If he pushes for a faster decision:**
"I want to be respectful of your timeline. I'm not dragging this out. I expect to have clarity within two weeks."

**Do NOT:**
- Mention other companies by name
- Negotiate comp
- Make it sound like you're shopping the offer
- Commit to a start date

## Research-Backed Negotiation Notes

**Key stats ([The Interview Guys meta-analysis](https://blog.theinterviewguys.com/we-reviewed-every-salary-negotiation-study/)):**
- People who negotiate get 18.83% more on average
- 66% of people who try get what they asked for
- 90% of employers are open to negotiating
- 85% of people who counter get at least some of what they asked for
- Excessive gratitude or accommodation signals low self-worth. Be warm but don't over-thank.

**Anchoring:** Don't state a number in a vacuum. Frame with market data: "Based on levels.fyi and comparable Staff roles in Bay Area aerospace, the range is typically $X-Y." ([Levels.fyi Archer data](https://www.levels.fyi/companies/archer/salaries))

**"Firm on goal, flexible on path":** Be clear about what you need (NYC, sign-on) but open to how it gets done (travel cadence, timing, structure).

**Remote negotiation framing ([HBR](https://hbr.org/2021/07/how-to-negotiate-a-remote-work-arrangement)):**
- Frame as employer benefit, not personal convenience. "I can be more effective on deep technical work from home, and on-site weeks would be focused on collaboration and hardware."
- Show concrete proof you deliver remotely (2x founder, shipped products across distributed teams).
- Anticipate that this may take multiple conversations. Dennis may need to check with leadership.
- Get the final arrangement in writing as part of the offer letter.

**Comp data points:**
- Archer posted range for this role: $170-215K base
- Offer came in at $230K base (above posted range, good sign)
- Shield AI Staff range: $228-342K
- Archer median SWE on levels.fyi: $198K TC (but this is non-staff level)
- 6figr reports Archer range $206-274K overall

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't mention specific competing companies by name
- Don't negotiate multiple things at once in the same breath. Lead with location, settle it, then move to comp if needed.
- Don't frame anything as an ultimatum. "I'd love to make this work" not "I can't accept unless..."
- Don't negotiate on the Friday call.
